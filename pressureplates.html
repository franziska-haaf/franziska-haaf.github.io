Pressure plates

An ambient interface for connecting people at meetups.

Problem

The social component is an important factor in Meetups. The majority of attendees value networking and want to build and expand social networks (source). But there are people at Meetups who do not actively participate in the social part of the Meetup. It was found that individuals who do not integrate often instead engage on their own and stay somewhat apart from the action, isolating themselves even more from the Meetup situation. These initial, social barriers should be overcome with the help of an interface, thereby bringing users into contact with other people.

Mission

Connect people who do not actively participate in the social events at Meetups by using an ambient interface.

Role

Master's student at the LMU in collaboration with Netlight

Timeline

November 2019 - June 2020

Methods

RtD | observations | spatial analysis | workshops | expert interviews | personas | user flows | (rapid) prototyping | guerrilla testing | qualitative user testing

Tools

Arduino | Electronics | Adobe Illustrator | Laser Cutter | 3D printer

About the Pressure plates

The prototype was developed in the context of my master thesis with the topic 'Examine the use of unobtrusive, interactive interfaces in a semi-public meetup situation'. The goal of the thesis was to explore the use of unobtrusive, interactive interfaces in semi-public meetup situations. The work was done with the research-through-design approach using iterative prototyping. A pair of interactive floor plates was iteratively created, offering a game for people interested in it.

The floor plates can be categorized as an ambient interface.

Research

The situation 'person does not integrate' was selected on the basis of a situation room analysis and a creative workshop using the lotus flower method.

Description of the rooms

Three spaces were evaluated. Netlight's previous Meetup event space, Netlight's future Meetup event space currently under renovation, and AWS (Amazon Web Services) event space in the Munich office.

There are sitting areas in all rooms that enable visitors to retreat. The spaces have kitchen areas that provided drinks and food. The majority of visitors spent their time standing in the area of the kitchen. For talks that took place during the rest of the Meetup, chairs facing the projection area were available to the visitors.

Meetup observations

A total of 4 Qualitative Observations were conducted in the Meetup spaces described. The aim is to identify and select a specific situation on the basis of which iterative prototypical approaches for unobtrusive interactive interfaces in Meetup situations are tested.

It could be observed that the crowd at the Meetups is spread out in the room. Seating, foosball, and food areas are all used. Conversation groups form, and at Meetups hosted by Netlight, single people are approached by hosts and initiated into conversation (with other people).

In contrast, at the AWS Meetup, some participating individuals who were supposedly alone engage in themselves, as no organizers jumped into the role of 'matchmakers'. These isolated individuals sat on armchairs or sofas away from the hustle and bustle, eating and drinking. A more frequently observed behavior was how isolated participants looked around, searching for a familiar face, strolling between the discussion groups, only to be again on their own at other quieter spots. This gave the impression of introverted personalities who felt uncomfortable placing themselves in the center of the activity or seeking direct contact with strangers. These participants could very well be picked up and engaged with the space , especially in quieter areas such as seating areas, and ideally brought into contact with other people.

Lotus Flower Method

The 'Lotus Flower Method', or 'Lotus Blossom Technique', is a creative method designed for groups and is used to deepen different solutions to a defined situation (for example, a problem or an idea). The method can be executed with small sticky notes.

In this process, a situation is placed as the center of a 3x3 matrix of sticky notes to generate ideas for a solution approach. The surrounding sticky notes are still blank. These sticky notes are now filled by the group with ideas on how to approach the situation. Subsequently, the (selected) ideas generated are placed in their own 3x3 matrices as a center point and again the empty slips of paper around them are filled with ideas. This process can now be repeated again if necessary.

The Lotus Flower method was conducted at Netlight with seven participants, including people with UX backgrounds and people who plan and/or host Meetups.

Since it was noticeable in the previous Meetup observations that there were visitors who did not actively participate in the social events of the Meetup, the situation was defined here as 'person does not integrate'. The method was used to find solutions to the problem of how to resolve this situation with the help of unobtrusive interactive interfaces.

The most interesting, and therefore most thoroughly considered by the method, were the ideas 'Laser pointer', 'Quiz' and 'Dance Dance Revolution with light fields on the floor'.

The result at the end of the workshop.

In the course of the workshop, participants also debated the situation 'person does not integrate', which was specified for the workshop. The participants were not asked to do this. In doing so, the recruiters participating in the workshop informed about the situation that they regularly put people in contact with each other at Meetups and thus integrate them through outside intervention. This further confirmed the situation assumed by the Meetup observations.

Conception

Inspiration

In the Lotus Flower Method workshop, the idea was compared to 'Dance Dance Revolution.' This is a dance game, which is operated with plates on the floor which show arrows in different directions. These arrows must then be activated with the plates when shown on the connected display. Another comparison that came up was the German children's TV show 'Tabaluga TV' where there is a memory task with ice floes on the floor that light up.

Persona

Based on the observations and feedback from the organizers, two personas were developed. For the prototype, the second persona 'Kumar Rani' is particularly relevant and is considered the target group.

Prototyping

The prototype 'pressure plate' was refined over several iterations. The first iteration mainly tested whether users understood the interaction itself. In the second iteration, the prototype was revised to appear more stable, provide ambient light, and present an icon to indicate use. In addition, a duplicate was created to implement the intended use of a game. In the third iteration, the panels were made of metal and now communicate with each other using the UDP network protocol to enable the aforementioned game. In the fourth and fifth iterations, customizations for better affordance, such as icons and a score display of the game state represented by LEDs, were added.

First Prototype

Two wooden plates were covered with aluminum foil, and a spacer between them ensured that the plates only touched when pressure was applied, thus closing the circuit and causing an LED to light up.

Guerilla Tests revealed that some users were afraid of damaging the Interface. Since the parts of the electronics were freely visible next to the plate and this seemed to unsettle the users, the plate was modified in a further step so that the LEDs could be stowed away in a hole on the underside of the prototype. With the help of a Plexiglas plate, which was also mounted on the underside, an ambient lighting effect was created around the printing plate.

With regard to the selected Meetup situation and the desired purpose there - bringing participants who are not yet integrated closer together socially and communicatively - it was also decided to create two panels that can communicate with each other. In addition, a meaningful use case still needs to be found instead of the individual LEDs.

Second Prototype

Based on the results and impressions of the guerrilla tests, the second iteration of the prototype underwent several adjustments: Two panels in a larger format were produced and the light was installed on the side to maintain the ambient lighting effect. The parts were made using a laser cutter and engraved with a light bulb symbol.

ESP8266 microcontrollers were used to enable the boards to communicate via wireless LAN. A fundamental goal for this iteration of the prototype was to improve affordance - that is, to find ways to make the plates' modes of interaction easier to understand and to make users less reluctant to interact with them.

The building plan of the second prototype. All parts have been created in Adobe Illustrator and cut with a laser cutter.

The second prototype without the upper plate that closes the circuit upon interaction. The upper plate with the icon also has a layer of aluminum on the other side. A foam layer on the edge maintains a distance between the plates in the rest position.

The microcontroller and the other electronics are hidden in the little wooden box. Pressure through the foot causes the color of the outer light strip to change.

Third Prototype

Based on the experience with the second iteration of the prototype, it was decided for the next iteration to create the plates out of metal. The aim was to make the plates appear more high-quality, more stable and less susceptible to dirt, thus counteracting inhibitions among users. The construction plan for this prototype is the same as for the previous iteration. The intended use is a game in which two users each operate a plate. Thanks to the Wifi-enabled ESP8266 microcontrollers, the boards can communicate with each other via the UDP protocol in a shared Wifi network. The LEDs of both plates display different colors in a random rotation. If both plates show the same color, the users have to actuate their respective plate as fast as possible. The user who presses his or her plate faster wins the game. The plates show a light animation for the winner and for the loser.

The plates were again created in a larger format, so that in this iteration people with larger feet can also press the pressure surface with their whole foot. The microcontrollers are placed at a greater distance from the plates thanks to longer cables.

Testing

Guerilla Testing

The first prototype was tested with Guerrilla Testing. For this purpose, the interface was laid out in the common area of the Netlight company and kept under observation. People were not actively made aware of the board or persuaded to interact.

Some people interacted freely with the Interface and activated it with their foot without instruction. Some people were afraid of damaging the interface. This led to a further modification of the interface.

Further guerrilla tests showed that the plate was partially not triggered because the non-clickable edge was in the way. This suggested that the plate was not big enough. Some users also pointed out that a hint on which interaction to perform would be helpful for them.

The guerilla tests have also shown that people present did not immediately take notice of the printing plates, but only on the third or fourth occasion. One reason for this could be that people in this specific work environment have little need to look down when communicating or carrying out their activities, but instead direct their gaze to coworkers or workstations. However, in the previously described meetup situation that is the focus of this paper, it is precisely this placement on the floor that turns into an advantage: presumably, a characteristic of our user group is a limited or differently oriented radius of vision because they are sitting, reading informational materials from the event or on their smartphones, or looking at the premises. This is in contrast to those who have already made a social connection and are communicating with other participants.

Guerilla Testing with the second prototype showed that this prototype also had the problem that the pressure area of the plate is somewhat too small when users want to place their whole foot on it to trigger it. The ambient light proved to be effective and increased the perceptibility of the LEDs. It also became clear that users are still afraid of damaging the plate when they step on it.

The prototype was later set up so that the wiring could be hidden under a floor flap containing the power outlets.

User testing

After the first of the following user tests, the plates were slightly adjusted to solve mechanical difficulties. During the usage study, it was found that the plate surface on which users applied pressure with their foot could slip inside the surrounding frame and unintentionally close the circuit by touching the frame. This regularly triggered a pressure signal that was not caused by the users. The edge of the pressure area was therefore insulated with non-conductive adhesive tape.

Due to the Corona measures, no qualitative user tests could be carried out in real Meetup situations. Therefore, qualitative single and double tests were used, which were feasible according to the measures. At the beginning, before the exploration with the prototypes, a questionnaire was filled out together with the participants. Afterwards, the users were allowed to explore the panels without further instruction.

The median values of the completed questionnaires as part of the user testing. (1 = very low, 20 = very high)

Needs further improvement
  • Clear icons or texts must prompt interaction
  • Mechanics must be as flawless as possible to avoid confusion and frustration
  • Dot display on top seems to distract from ambient light. Ambient light too unobtrusive. Light on top (covered with frosted glass) could be a solution.
  • Playing together is more fun, possibly construct game with multiple players per single board.
  • Interface can be difficult if not impossible to activate while sitting. People stand during use which makes use in sitting areas in front of sofas etc. not possible.
  • Large size sometimes causes people to stand with both feet on the plate and remain standing.
Positive findings
  • Concept of a flat object lying on the ground that offers interaction is known.
  • Interaction by foot is clear
  • Interface seems suitable as icebreaker for contacts with other people.

Future protoypes

Based on the results of the user tests, the following variants are proposed for further prototypes. These should be tested in future real Meetup situations, when this is possible again.

Smaller, no edge

The interface could be made smaller and without an edge. This could be made possible by placing the LEDs under the base plate. This should make it easier to activate and enable operation while sitting.

Same size, no edge

If the size of the interface is maintained, a separate circuit could be closed for each corner of the interface in a borderless implementation, through which the board offers multiple input possibilities. This would allow a single plate to be used by multiple players simultaneously. However, this could lead to the plate no longer behaving as an unobtrusive interface.

Ergonomic form

The interface could be designed in a (half) shoe shape similar to a foot pedal, as they are common in cars or for drums and sewing machines.
